#### 1. Towards Zero-Label Language Learning

> [1] Wang, Z., Yu, A. W., Firat, O., & Cao, Y. (2021). Towards zero-label language learning. *arXiv preprint arXiv:2109.09193*. [pdf](material\NLP\2109.09193.pdf)  [web](https://arxiv.org/abs/2109.09193)

The paper proposes a <span style='color:red'> zero-label learning </span>s procedure with <span style='color:red'> Unsupervised Data Generation (UDG) </span> to better utilize the capability of large pre-trained language models and by thus to avoid using artificially labeled datasets. Compared with few-shot inference models like *GPT*, the idea of *UDG* is few-shot data generation as a data augmentation method and train like a few-shot inference model.



For a labeled dataset $\mathcal{L}=\{(x^{i}, y^{i})\}_{i=1}^{n}$, two of the common training procedures are: (1) __Fine-tuning__: fine-rune a pre-training model with enormous amount of labeled data; (2) __few-short inference__: a resource-efficient method to allow model to infer correct label using only task descriptions and only few sample input-label pairs. The input to the model *M* consists of: task descriptions *T*, small set of *K* examples $\mathcal{L}_{few}=\{(x^{i}, y^{i})\}_{i=1}^{K}\subseteq\mathcal{L}$, and query example $x_{q}$, then, the model is trained to find most probable next text sequence $y_{q}$ as correct label:
$$
y_{q}=\underset{y}{argmax}P_{M}(y|[T, \mathcal{L}_{few}, x_{q}])
$$
On the other hand, __few-shot generation__ is designed to generate input *X* instead. The model is queried to generate $x_{g}$ corresponding to a pseudo label $\hat{y}_{g}$ consisted of a small set of *K* unlabeled examples $\mathcal{U}=\{x^{i}\}_{i=1}^{K}$ and a description:
$$
x_{g}\sim P_{M}(x|[T, \mathcal{U}, Des(\hat{y}_{g})])
$$
where $Des(\cdot)$ is a task-specific transformation function that maps a label class to natural language descriptions.



#### 2. PENS: A Dataset and Generic Framework for Personalized News Headline Generation

> [1] Ao, X., Wang, X., Luo, L., Qiao, Y., He, Q., & Xie, X. (2021, August). PENS: A Dataset and Generic Framework for Personalized News Headline Generation. In *Proceedings of the 59th Annual Meeting of the Association for Computational Linguistics and the 11th International Joint Conference on Natural Language Processing (Volume 1: Long Papers)* (pp. 82-92). [pdf](material\NLP\ACL_2021_Long\2021.acl-long.7.pdf)  [web](https://aclanthology.org/2021.acl-long.7/)  [github](https://msnews.github.io/pens.html)

The paper proposes a <span style='color:red'> personalized headline generator </span> which outputs user-specific news headlines using a <span style='color:red'> PENS (PErsonalized News headlineS) dataset</span>. The problem is solved by learning user preferences and using preference injections to generate personalized headlines. The goal is to generate balanced title that attracts enough attention from audience while not exaggerating information (which may cause negative effect). The datasets for this specific task is limited since it's difficult to prepare user-personalized headlines and evaluate their accuracy. The PENS dataset is publicized where the training set consists of 500,000 impressions over 445,765 users on more than one hundred thousand English news articles collected from Microsoft News, and test set contains 103 English native speakers’ click behaviors as well as their more than 20,000 manually-crafted personalized headlines of news articles on the same news corpus.



The problem of personalized news headline generation can be formalized as: for a user *u* with past click history $[c_{1}^{u}, c_{2}^{u},..., c_{N}^{u}]$ where *c* represents a headline the user clicked and each headline consists of a sequence of words $c=[w_{c_{1}}, w_{c_{2}}, ..., w_{c_{T}}]$ with maximum length *T*. The problem is to generate new headline $H_{v}^{u}=[y_{v_{1}}^{u}, y_{v_{2}}^{u}, ..., y_{v_{T}}^{u}]$ when the user is exposed to new news materials $v=[w_{v_{1}}, w_{v_{2}}, ..., w_{v_{n}}]$. Compared with personalized news recommendation tasks, where the goal is to assign news material with a click probability for specific users based on their past click history and solved by learning encoded user's personal preference and matching most probable future click, the outputs of personalized news headline generation are series of words based on the users' preference. There are similarities where the users' personal preferences are essential but with different objectives.



__Training set__: training set of PENS dataset consists of labeled samples of users'  impression logs, the format is [*uID*, *tmp*, *clkNews*, *uclkNews*, *clkedHis*], where *uID* is user ID. *tmp* stands for timestamps for the entry, *clkNews* and *uclkNews* are clicked/unclicked news respectively, *clkedHis* represents previously clicked news. The news are stored as news ID in the set and ordered by click time.

__Test set__: test set contains two parts of personalized news headline: (1) users select at least 50 attractive (to them) news headline-body pairs from total of 1000 such pairs; (2) users are asked to write another 200 their preferred news headlines given only the news body and examined by experts to retain only high quality headlines. Two parts are separately selected from news database to avoid interference.

__Headline Generator__: The structure of headline generator is a variant of transformer encoder and pointer network decoder. For the encoder, shown in above figure, given the candidate news body $v=[w_{v_{1}}, w_{v_{2}},...,w_{v_{n}}]$, the corresponding word embeddings $[e_{v_{1}},e_{v_{2}},...,e_{v_{n}}]\in R^{d_{w}}$ are fed to two-layer positional encoding where the first layer enhances word structure within entire news body and the second layer learn at sentence  level. The positional encodings are calculate as:
$$
PE_{(pos, 2i)}=sin(pos/10000^{2i/d_{w}})\\
PE_{(pos, 2i+1)}=cos(pos/10000^{2i/d_{w}})
$$
where *pos* represents the word position and *i* is the dimension. For the sentence level layer, suppose $W_{pos}\in R^{L\times d_{s}}$ is the position embedding matrix where *L* is the sentence length, and $d_{s}$ is the embedding size, then the *l*-th row of $W_{pos}$ stands for the corresponding positional embedding of the sentence. Then, word embedding containing positional information can be expressed as:
$$
e_{pos}'=(e_{pos}+PE_{pos})\oplus W_{pos}[l]
$$
And, multi-head self-attention mechanism is applied to get *encoder hidden state*, which can be calculated as $h_{i}=softmax(\dfrac{E'W_{i}^{Q}(E'W_{i}^{K})^{T}}{\sqrt{d_{k}}})E'W_{i}^{V}$$, $$i=1, 2, ..., k$ for *k* heads, where $d_{k}=\dfrac{d_{s}+d_{w}}{k}$ and $W_{i}^{Q}$, $W_{i}^{K}$, $W_{i}^{V}\in R^{(d_{s}+d_{w})\times d_{k}}$ are the query, key, value respectively. $E'$ represents the word sequence embeddings in the candidate news. Then the encoder hidden state can be concatenated as $h=h_{1}\oplus h_{2}...h_{k}$.

For decoder, the *decoder hidden state* $s_{t}$ at time *t* can be calculated  given input $x_{t}$. The decoder hidden state is then fed with encoder hidden state to calculate *attention distribution* $a_{t}$, which can expressed as $a_{t}=\mathcal{F}_{\theta}(h, s_{t})$. The function template for $\mathcal{F}_{\theta}$ is :
$$
\mathcal{F}_{\theta}(h, s_{t})=softmax(V_{att}^{T}\tanh(W_{h}h+W_{s}s_{t}+b_{att}))
$$
The *context vector* $c_{t}$, a fixed-length representation for the news body at time *t* can then be calculated as a weighted sum of the encoder hidden states over the attention distribution. Then, the *vocabulary distribution* is computed as $P_{vocab}(w_{t})=\tanh(V_{p}[s_{t};c_{t}]+b_{v})$. The vocabulary distribution stands for the probability distribution over all words to predict the word at time *t*.

A *pointer* $p_{gen}^{t}$ is then adopted to decide whether generate a word from vocabulary with probability determined by vocabulary distribution $P_{vocab}(w_{t})$ or copy a word from news body sampled from attention distribution $a_{t}$. The overall probability can be expressed as:
$$
P(w_{t})=p_{gen}^{t}P_{vocab}(w_{t})+(1-p_{gen}^{t})\sum_{j:w_{j}=w_{t}}a_{t, j}
$$
  where $P_{vocab}(w_{t})$ indicates $w_{t}$ out of vocabulary and $\sum_{j:w_{j}=w_{t}}a_{t, j}$ indicates $w_{t}$ out of news body. The pointer $p_{gen}^{t}$ can be calculated using $p_{gen}^{t}=\mathcal{T}_{\theta}(c_{t},s_{t}, x_{t})$ where $c_{t}$, $s_{t}$ and $x_{t}$ are context vector, decoder hidden state, decoder input respectively, and $\mathcal{T}_{\theta}$ follows the function template as attention distribution.



__Injecting User Preference__: Three methods are applied in the model to inject users' embedding (indicated in above figure): (1) use the user embedding *u* to initialize decoder hidden state $s_{0}$; (2) under assumption that users may interest in different paragraphs or words in different news body, user embedding *u* is used in attention distribution $a_{t}$ to personalize the attention on different words, by thus, attention distribution is re-formulated as $a_{t}=\mathcal{F}_{\theta}(h, s_{t}, u)$; (3) user embedding *u* is injected into the pointer where generating from vocabulary or selecting from news body is determined, and new pointer can be expressed as $p_{gen}^{t}=\mathcal{T}_{\theta}(c_{t},s_{t},x_{t},u)$.



#### 3. Enhancing Content Preservation in Text Style Transfer Using Reverse Attention and Conditional Layer Normalization

> [1] Lee, D., Tian, Z., Xue, L., & Zhang, N. L. (2021). Enhancing content preservation in text style transfer using reverse attention and conditional layer normalization. *arXiv preprint arXiv:2108.00449*. [pdf](material\NLP\ACL_2021_Long\2021.acl-long.8.pdf)  [web](https://aclanthology.org/2021.acl-long.8/)  [github](https://github.com/MovingKyu/RACoLN)

The <span style='color:red'>test style transfer</span> tasks intend to alter the style of sentences while retaining its content. Previous solutions are designed to covert sentences to content representations, remove style information and convert to another style using a decoder at the token level. And the token representations either contain content information or style information (not both), which for flat distributions (all tokens are assigned with similar attention scores), can results in inaccurate style information removal. The paper proposes a method by implicitly removing style information and better dynamically convert to another style. As proposed, in the paper, a token representation can contain both style information and content information, so, <span style='color:red'>reverse attention</span> is applied, where portion of style information a token contains is estimated. Thus, content information can be better retained. Furthermore, the target style is constructed with the help of building content-dependent style representations using <span style='color:red'>conditional layer normalization (CLN)</span>, which shapes content to fit in the target style distribution.

__Text Style Transfer Tasks__: The tasks can be formulated as: for a training dataset $\mathcal{D}=\{(x_{i},s_{i})_{i=1}^{N}\}$ where $x_{i}$ refers to a sentence and $s_{i}$ is a label represents the sentence style, the goal is to learn a model $\hat{x}_{\hat{s}}=f_{\theta}(x_{i},\hat{s})$ that given input sentence $x_{i}$ and a target style label $\hat{s}$, the model can output a sentence $\hat{x}_{\hat{s}}$ with the target style while retaining all content information. 



__Model Architecture__: The model is trained in a unsupervised fashion where the ground truth sentence $x_{\hat{s}}$ is entirely unknown and in a adversarial style where a style classifier $s=C(x)$ is pre-trained (before model training and frozen during training) to identify the style of sentence $x$. The model then becomes to learn a $\hat{x}_{\hat{s}}=f_{\theta}(x, \hat{s})$ such that $C(\hat{x}_{\hat{s}})=\hat{s}$. The architecture (shown in above figure) contains a *decoder*, a *styler* and a *decoder*. The decoder removes the style information (left with style-independent, content only representation $z_{x}$) and assign attention scores to the tokens, the styler takes the content representations $z_{x}$ and target style $\hat{s}$ to generate content-related style representation $z_{\hat{s}}$. Then, the decoder takes the style representation $z_{\hat{s}}$ and content representation $z_{x}$ to generate the target sequence $\hat{x}_{\hat{s}}$.

__Encoder__: The encoder consists of two parts: (1) *Style Marker Module*, which is a one-layer bidirectional pre-trained GRU layer with attention, can calculate the proportion of style information in the input tokens. The corresponding attention scores can be calculated as:
$$
v_{t}=\tanh(W_{w}h_{t}+b_{w})\\
\alpha_{t}=\dfrac{\exp(v_{t}^{T}u/\tau)}{\sum_{t=1}^{T}\exp(v_{t}^{T}u/\tau)}
$$
where $h_{t}$ is the output of pre-trained GRU layer at time *t*, and the attention is normalized with softmax function. During the pre-training phase of style marker module, the attention scores are further fed as weights to hidden representations $h_{t}$ and a fully-connected layer, which can be expressed as:
$$
o=\sum_{t=1}^{T}\alpha_{t}h_{t}\\
p=softmax(W_{c}o+b_{c})
$$
In this fashion, the process of calculating attention scores can be optimized and this module can learn what token representations are critical for styles to what extent. After pre-training, the fully-connected layer is removed since only attention score computation layers are need.

(2) *Reverse Attention*: From style marker module, the style information of token representations are generated, represented by attention scores $\alpha_{t}$. This layer is designed to suppress style information and retrieve only content representations. Intuitively, since $\alpha_{t}$ is normalized ($\sum_{t=1}^{T}\alpha_{t}=1$), defining $\tilde{\alpha}_{t}=1-\alpha_{t}$ as reverse attention can retrieve the content information. The reverse attention layer can be expressed as $\tilde{e}_{t}=\tilde{\alpha}_{t}e_{t}$, where $e_{t}=E(x_{t})$ is the token representation. The reverse attention is then fed into a bidirectional GRU layer $z_{x}=bidirectionalGRU(\tilde{e})$ to get style-independent, content only representations $z_{x}$.

__Styler__: The styler is designed to generate content-dependent style representations given input of style-dependent content representations $z_{x}$. To unify the dimension of inputs, the content representations are first fed to a fully-connected layer $\tilde{z}_{x}=W_{z}z_{x}+b_{z}$, then fed to a conditional layer normalization layer:
$$
z_{\hat{s}}=CLN(\tilde{z}_{x}; \hat{s})=\gamma^{\hat{s}}\odot N(\tilde{z}_{x})+\beta^{\hat{s}}\\
N(\tilde{z}_{x})=\dfrac{\tilde{z}_{x}-\mu}{\sigma}
$$
where $\mu$, $\sigma$ are the mean, standard deviation of inputs respectively and $\gamma^{\hat{s}}$, $\beta^{\hat{s}}$ are the style-related parameters (learnt independently among styles). The idea is that layer normalization can shift the content representations to a target style distribution while still retain content information. By thus, the style representations generated is dependent on the content information. Furthermore, to remove the impact of style information from previous layers through backpropagation, the gradient is stopped before fed into the styler.

__Decoder__: The decoder is a one-layer GRU that takes content representation $z_{x}$ and content-dependent style representation $z_{\hat{s}}$ to generate sentence for desired style $\hat{x}_{\hat{s}}\sim Dec(z_{x}, z_{\hat{s}})=p_{D}(\hat{x}_{\hat{s}}|z_{x},z_{\hat{s}})$.

__Pre-trained Style Classifier__: As mentioned above, there exists a pre-trained style classifier $C(x)$ that can classify the style category given a input sentence. The structure of this style classifier is same as the pre-training style marker module (fully-connected layer appended), which is trained by cross entropy loss: $\mathcal{L}_{pre}=-\mathbb{E}_{(x, s)\sim\mathcal{D}}[\log p_{C}(s|x_{s})]$. The classifier is frozen during the training phase.
